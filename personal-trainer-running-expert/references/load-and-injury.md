# Load management and injury red flags

How to grow load safely and when to pull back. This is coaching judgment, not medical
advice — refer to a clinician when red flags appear.

## Growing load
- Nudge weekly volume modestly (~5–10%, individualized) — it's a guide, not a rule.
- Don't spike volume and intensity in the same block.
- Use a build/deload rhythm (often ~3:1) so adaptation can catch up.
- Avoid sharp jumps in acute load relative to what the athlete has been doing — a sudden
  big week is where injuries cluster.

## Monitoring from CoachBridge data
- **Under-recovery signal:** resting HR drifting up **and** HRV trending below its baseline
  band **and** poor sleep, sustained over several days → reduce load now.
- **Fatigue signal:** elevated HR (or RPE) for the usual pace; paces slipping at the same
  effort.
- Read trends over days, not single readings — Apple HRV (SDNN) is sampled sporadically.
- A single bad night isn't a red flag; a sustained pattern is.

## Red flags — be cautious, advise rest or a professional
- Sharp or localized pain; pain that changes your gait; pain that worsens during or after running.
- Swelling, or pain that doesn't settle with a few easy days.
- Systemic: sustained low HRV + high resting HR + poor sleep, illness, or unusual persistent fatigue.

## Niggle protocol
1. Drop to easy-only or cross-train; cut volume and intensity.
2. Reassess over a few days. Improving → ease back gradually. Not improving, or any red
   flag above → rest and recommend a professional evaluation.

## Return to running (after time off or illness)
Ramp back conservatively. Restore easy volume first, reintroduce intensity last, and expect
it to take a little time — coming back too fast re-injures.
